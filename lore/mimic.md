# Mimic — D&D Lore Research

## Status Note

The Mimic **IS in the D&D 5e SRD** (Systems Reference Document). It appears in the SRD under its standard stat block and description. All mechanical details below can be used freely under the Open Game License / Creative Commons SRD. Lore beyond the stat block (personality depth, ecology elaborations, specific named variants) draws on:
- *Monster Manual* (5e, 2014) — the richest single source
- *Volo's Guide to Monsters* (5e, 2016) — adds ecology, variants, and the "mimic colony" concept
- Earlier editions (1e, 2e, 3.5e) for historical context
- Design commentary and published sourcebooks

## 1. Origin / History

### First Appearance and Authorship

The Mimic first appeared in **Supplement II: Blackmoor** (1975), created by Dave Arneson — one of the two co-creators of D&D alongside Gary Gygax. It was then formally statted in the **AD&D 1st Edition Monster Manual** (1977). From the very beginning, the Mimic was conceived as a **dungeon predator that disguises itself as inanimate objects**, specifically treasure chests.

It has appeared in every major edition of D&D:
- **OD&D (Supplement II: Blackmoor, 1975):** First appearance
- **AD&D 1e Monster Manual (1977):** Fully statted, treasure-chest focus
- **AD&D 2e (1989):** Retained, given more ecological depth in *Monstrous Compendium*
- **D&D 3.5e:** Updated stat block, shapechanger subtype
- **D&D 4e:** Redefined as a solo lurker, kept the object-disguise concept
- **D&D 5e Monster Manual (2014):** Current canonical version — shapechanger with adhesive, pseudopod, and bite attacks. **Included in the SRD.**

### No Real-World Roots — A Pure Dungeon Predator

Unlike the Troll (Norse folklore filtered through Poul Anderson) or the Basilisk (classical antiquity), the Mimic has **no mythological precursor.** It is a pure dungeon-ecology invention — a creature designed to answer the question "what if the dungeon itself was trying to kill you?" The Mimic is the embodiment of the fundamental D&D lesson: *never assume anything is safe, not even the furniture.*

There are some loose thematic echoes in real-world nature (mimic octopuses, anglerfish lures, carnivorous pitcher plants that attract prey), but these are convergent concepts rather than sources. The D&D Mimic was invented as a game design element first and given biology second.

Because the Mimic has no real-world folklore roots, the **"Origin" reveal section is not applicable** for this creature. Its story is one of dungeon ecology, not mythology.

## 2. Appearance

### Default Form: The Treasure Chest

The Mimic's most iconic disguise is a **treasure chest** — and for good reason. In a dungeon full of adventurers hunting for gold, nothing is more reliably attractive than a chest sitting in a room, seemingly unguarded. The Mimic exploits exactly this greed.

In chest form, a Mimic is essentially indistinguishable from a real chest:
- **Correct proportions:** Roughly 2-4 feet wide, 1.5-2 feet deep, 2 feet tall
- **Correct texture and color:** It can replicate wood grain, iron banding, hinge details, lock mechanisms, and surface patina
- **Correct weight illusion:** It sits heavily, doesn't shift around, doesn't respond to nudges
- **No seams showing biology:** Breathing, heartbeat, internal movement — all suppressed and hidden
- **Can mimic the smell of old wood and metal** — some sources suggest scent-matching is imperfect, and experienced adventurers notice something "wrong" without being able to name it

The disguise is visual and tactile but not magical — it is a **biological shapeshifting ability**, not an illusion. This matters: spells that detect magic won't find a Mimic. Spells that detect life might, but most adventurers don't think to use them on furniture.

### True Form

When a Mimic attacks — or when it drops its disguise — its true nature is revealed:

- **Body:** Amorphous, blob-like mass with no fixed anatomy. The core is roughly the size of the object it was imitating.
- **Pseudopods:** Extends thick, rubbery limbs from its mass — these are used for grabbing, striking, and holding prey. They emerge suddenly from what looked like solid wood or metal.
- **Mouth:** A large, tooth-filled maw opens somewhere on the body — typically on the "lid" of the chest. The teeth are real bone-like structures grown from its biology. Rows of irregular, sharp, backwards-curving teeth to prevent prey from pulling free.
- **Eyes:** One or more eyes appear — simple, yellowish, alien. They have no lids and stare with an unsettling flat hunger.
- **Color:** The true body is typically a mottled **grey-brown or tan**, similar to the color of rock or old wood. Somewhat translucent in parts. Wet-looking.
- **Surface texture:** When not mimicking, the surface is rough and slightly sticky — covered in the adhesive secretion it uses to trap prey.
- **Size:** Medium creature in 5e. The chest form is also roughly Medium scale. Mimics can grow larger (see Variants), but the core creature is roughly the size of a large suitcase.

### The Moment of Reveal

There is a split second between a Mimic dropping its disguise and full attack mode — and it is described across multiple editions as deeply alarming. The chest suddenly **sprouts limbs, a mouth tears open across what looked like the lid, teeth appear where wood grain was, and something grabs you.** This moment — the uncanny valley violation of furniture suddenly becoming alive — is central to why the Mimic has endured for 50 years.

## 3. Behavior

### Intelligence and Personality

The Mimic has **INT 5** in 5e — below average human intelligence, but significantly smarter than an animal. This puts it in interesting territory: it can plan a basic ambush, remember prey behavior, and understand that patience is rewarded, but it cannot plot, converse meaningfully, or pursue complex strategies.

Some editions give certain Mimics higher intelligence. *Volo's Guide* introduces the **Mimic Colony** concept (see Ecology), and those collective Mimics have shared intelligence that approaches sentience.

A key behavioral quirk noted in *Volo's Guide*: **Mimics with high enough intelligence can learn to speak**, using the languages of creatures they've consumed. An old, well-fed Mimic might speak broken Common or Dwarvish — knowledge absorbed along with its meals. This detail is non-SRD but deeply evocative.

### The Hunt: Pure Ambush Predation

The Mimic is one of the purest sit-and-wait predators in D&D. Its hunting behavior:

1. **Find a location** where prey will inevitably appear — treasure rooms, dungeon corridors, campsites
2. **Select a disguise** appropriate to that location (chest, barrel, door, weapon rack, book)
3. **Wait** — potentially for weeks or months without moving, without feeding
4. **Trigger** when prey touches the object or comes within grabbing range
5. **Immobilize** with adhesive and pseudopod grapple simultaneously
6. **Bite and digest** — the prey is anchored and cannot pull free

The Mimic does not chase prey. It does not pursue adventurers down hallways. Once prey escapes its immediate reach, the Mimic will generally **reset** — return to disguise form and wait again. The hunt is defined entirely by the quality of the disguise and the location choice, not by speed or aggression.

### Patience as Survival Strategy

The Mimic's patience is one of its most remarkable traits. It can remain motionless in disguise form for **weeks or months** without feeding. During this time, it effectively ceases most biological activity — a kind of ambush-torpor. This is consistent with real-world sit-and-wait predators (some species of spider, anglerfish, certain pit vipers) but taken to an extreme for a creature the size of a chest.

The patience is not intelligent in the sense of "planning" — it is biological drive. The Mimic is not bored. It is not watching for opportunities. It is dormant and triggered by contact, like a biological trap. When something touches it, instinct fires.

### Territoriality and Movement

Mimics are **semi-territorial but mobile**. They will relocate when:
- A location stops producing prey (adventurers learn to avoid that room)
- Better locations become available
- They are driven out by other predators

Relocation is done at night or during quiet periods — a Mimic moving through a dungeon is one of the more uncanny possibilities. It can move at full speed (15 ft.) in its true form. Whether it passes any observer depends on whether that observer is paying attention to apparently empty corridors.

Mimics are drawn to **treasure rooms, vaults, and places of obvious wealth** — not because they understand wealth, but because they have learned (through experience or instinct) that these locations attract prey reliably. A Mimic in a plain empty corridor rarely feeds. A Mimic in a room full of treasure chests — indistinguishable from the real ones — eats very well.

### Disposition Toward Non-Prey

Mimics are not mindlessly hostile. They target prey they can consume. A creature that is clearly too large to eat, or a very powerful creature, may be ignored rather than attacked — the Mimic's biology seems to include a rudimentary threat-assessment drive.

Some higher-intelligence Mimics develop what *Volo's Guide* describes as **"object possessiveness"** — they become attached to their disguise location and will attack anything near it on principle, not just potential prey. This makes them more dangerous as lair guards but also more predictable.

## 4. Habitat

### Primary: Underground / Dungeon Environments

Mimics are quintessential dungeon creatures. They require:
- **Enclosed spaces** with defined entry/exit points (so prey has limited escape options)
- **Other objects of the same type** nearby, for camouflage (a single chest in an empty room is suspicious; a chest among six other chests is invisible)
- **Regular prey traffic** — dungeons, ruins, vaults, tombs, abandoned keeps

**They are rarely found in the open wilderness.** A Mimic in a forest has no good disguise options and cannot ambush effectively. In an urban setting, they occasionally infiltrate warehouses, markets, or docks — hiding as crates or barrels among legitimate cargo. Urban Mimics are rare but more dangerous in some ways because prey doesn't expect them there.

### Preferred Location Types

- **Treasure rooms and vaults** — highest prey density
- **Entry corridors** — mimicking doors or large locked chests
- **Armory rooms** — mimicking weapon racks, shield stands, armor displays
- **Libraries** — some large Mimics mimic bookshelves or reading chairs
- **Temples and crypts** — altars, sarcophagi, offering boxes (Mimics in crypts mimic sarcophagi — one of the more horrifying applications)
- **Taverns and inns** — rare, but some Mimics infiltrate common spaces disguised as furniture

### Do They Build Lairs?

Mimics **do not build or maintain lairs** in the conventional sense. They are the lair. Wherever they choose to sit is their territory for the duration. The closest thing to a "Mimic lair" is a location where a Mimic colony has taken up residence — in which case, the entire room may be mimics (see Ecology).

### The Dungeon Ecosystem Niche

In dungeon ecology, Mimics occupy a very specific niche: **the passive checkpoint.** They don't generate territory conflicts with active predators (trolls, owlbears, undead) because they don't compete for the same kind of prey encounters. A troll ambushes prey in corridors through active pursuit; a Mimic waits. They can share a dungeon without much conflict, and Mimics sometimes set up in the aftermath of active predators — near where other monsters leave remains, since adventurers drawn to fight those monsters will also encounter the Mimic.

## 5. Diet

### What They Eat

Mimics are **carnivores** that consume the entire body of their prey. This includes:
- Flesh, bone, leather, cloth, and some metals (digestive acids are powerful)
- **Humanoids are primary prey** — not because Mimics specifically hunt humanoids, but because dungeons are primarily visited by humanoids
- Small animals that pass near them (rats, bats, cave creatures)
- Other small monsters

**They do not eat plants, fungi, or most minerals.** Their digestive system is optimized for protein-rich organic matter.

### Feeding Mechanics

The Mimic does not have a conventional stomach. It **envelops prey** — the body flows around the victim, and digestive enzymes break down the organic matter over hours. This is similar (conceptually) to how a real-world amoeba consumes food at a microscopic scale, but scaled up enormously.

A Mimic can fully consume a human-sized prey creature in approximately **8-12 hours**. During this time, it is at its most vulnerable — it is less mobile, its disguise may be imperfect (visible bulges or seams), and it is somewhat sluggish.

**What gets left behind:** Metal items, gems, and non-organic materials are not fully digested. A Mimic that has consumed a well-equipped adventurer may have coins, rings, and weapon fragments partially embedded in or near its body. This is both a clue to its nature (loot near a "chest" that shouldn't be there) and a reason some treasure hunters specifically seek out Mimic lairs — old Mimics in heavily trafficked dungeons accumulate significant wealth over their lifetimes.

### Feeding Frequency

A Mimic can survive **weeks to months without feeding** due to its ambush-torpor biology. After a successful kill, a well-fed Mimic may remain dormant in the same spot for an extended period — not needing to eat and therefore not bothering to attack smaller or more dangerous prey that passes by.

Starvation drives aggression: a Mimic that hasn't fed for a very long time will be more trigger-happy, attacking prey it might otherwise assess as too dangerous.

## 6. Abilities / Combat

### SRD Stat Block (5e)

- **CR:** 2 (450 XP)
- **Type:** Monstrosity (Shapechanger)
- **AC:** 12 (natural armor)
- **HP:** 58 (9d8 + 18)
- **Speed:** 15 ft.
- **STR** 17, **DEX** 12, **CON** 15, **INT** 5, **WIS** 13, **CHA** 8
- **Skills:** Stealth +5
- **Damage Immunities:** Acid
- **Condition Immunities:** Prone (when in object form, it's already on the ground)
- **Senses:** Darkvision 60 ft.
- **Languages:** —
- **Shapechanger:** Can polymorph into an object or back into its true, amorphous form. Statistics don't change between forms. Doesn't revert if killed.

### Adhesive (Object Form Only)

**The Mimic's single most tactically devastating ability.** When in object form, the Mimic's surface is coated with an extraordinarily powerful biological adhesive.

- Any creature that **touches the Mimic** (including picking it up, touching its surface to examine it) is **immediately grappled** (escape DC 13)
- The creature is **restrained** while grappled this way — it cannot move freely
- The adhesive is biological — it cannot be loosened by normal solvents in most editions. *Universal Solvent*, fire damage, or the Mimic's own "release" (which it does when it dies or chooses) are the ways to break free
- A creature that uses a weapon on the Mimic while adjacent risks the **weapon sticking** — the weapon is now adhered to the Mimic's body
- Multiple creatures can be stuck simultaneously

This ability is the reason the Mimic is so dangerous at CR 2. A fighter who tries to open the chest is immediately stuck, cannot move away, and is now being bitten at close range while their friends either try to free them (risking getting stuck too) or attack from range.

### Pseudopod

- **Attack bonus:** +5 to hit
- **Damage:** 1d8 + 3 bludgeoning damage
- **Special:** On hit, target is also subjected to the Adhesive (see above) if not already stuck
- Used in true form as the Mimic's reach attack — can extend pseudopods 5-10 feet
- The pseudopod can emerge from any surface of the Mimic's body — including from what looked like the hinges or lock of a chest

### Bite

- **Attack bonus:** +5 to hit
- **Damage:** 1d8 + 3 piercing + 1d8 acid damage
- The acid component is notable: it applies ongoing damage and can dissolve equipment
- The bite is the close-range finishing attack — used on stuck prey that cannot escape
- The backwards-curving teeth mean that pulling away from a bite attempt actually drives the teeth deeper

### Shapeshifting Range — What Can a Mimic Imitate?

This is a frequently asked question, and the answer has evolved across editions:

**Standard 5e SRD:** The Mimic can take the shape of **any object** it has observed. The object must be roughly its own size (Medium). This means:
- Treasure chests — iconic, classic
- Doors (a door-mimic is among the most terrifying: the whole door grabs you when you touch the handle)
- Barrels, crates, boxes
- Large books (a Mimic posing as a tome on a shelf)
- Armor on a stand
- A throne or large chair
- A weapon rack
- An altar

**What it cannot do by default:**
- **Mimic a person** — not in 5e. The Mimic is a Monstrosity, not a Doppelganger. It mimics objects, not creatures. A Medium-sized blob cannot convincingly replicate a humanoid's moving, talking behavior. Some editions introduced "Greater Mimics" with more capability, but standard Mimics do not mimic people.
- **Mimic a whole room** — a single standard Mimic cannot. However, a **Mimic Colony** can collectively cover an entire room (see Ecology). The *Volo's Guide* variant presents a colony that collectively forms walls, floors, and ceiling — an entire room is the Mimic. This is one of the most horrifying concepts in D&D.
- **Mimic smells perfectly** — the disguise is primarily visual and tactile. Some lore suggests scent-matching is approximate, and creatures with keen smell (dogs, rangers) might detect something wrong.

**The object must be:**
- Approximately the right size (Medium, roughly 2-8 cubic feet)
- A solid, non-living object
- Something the Mimic has had time to study (it doesn't instantly know how to mimic something brand-new)

### Acid Immunity

Mimics are completely immune to acid damage. This reflects their biology — their own digestive enzymes are acid-based, and their body is adapted to constant acid exposure. Pouring acid on a Mimic to free a stuck ally does nothing to the Mimic.

### False Appearance (Object Form)

While motionless in object form, the Mimic is **indistinguishable from an ordinary object.** This is the passive stealth ability. Investigation checks to detect the Mimic are made against its Stealth (+5), and even a successful check merely reveals "something is wrong" — not necessarily "it's a Mimic."

## 7. Weaknesses

1. **Low speed (15 ft.):** Once a Mimic drops its disguise, it is **slow**. A healthy creature can simply run. The Mimic's entire predatory strategy depends on the prey already being stuck. If prey escapes before getting glued, the fight turns entirely against the Mimic.

2. **Adhesive backfire:** The adhesive can work against the Mimic. If multiple creatures pile on from range (bows, spells), the Mimic cannot easily reach them. The stuck creature is taking damage but is also absorbing the Mimic's melee actions — the rest of the party has a free attack window.

3. **Fire:** Not a formal weakness in 5e mechanics, but fire is highly effective against the adhesive in many editions and lore descriptions. More importantly, fire will generally deter the Mimic from staying in one place — it won't risk being burned to hold a position.

4. **Low intelligence:** INT 5 means the Mimic cannot adapt to unexpected tactics, cannot coordinate with allies, and cannot predict multi-step strategies. It has one trick. If that trick fails (prey doesn't touch it, prey uses ranged combat from the start), it has no backup plan.

5. **Vulnerability during digestion:** A feeding Mimic is slower and its disguise is imperfect. This is the optimal time to encounter and destroy one — though recognizing a "lumpy, seam-showing chest" in a dungeon is not a skill most adventurers develop until it's too late.

6. **Detection by smell:** Creatures with keen smell (dogs, gnolls, rangers) have advantage on checks to notice something wrong about the "object." Old Mimics smell faintly of decay and stomach acid, especially when they haven't perfectly cleaned up after a meal.

7. **No ranged attacks:** The Mimic cannot do anything to creatures attacking from a distance. A party that maintains range and attacks with bows or spells will kill a Mimic without any adhesive risk, provided they notice it before touching it.

8. **The "dead giveaway" check:** The object is always worth investigating carefully. Experienced adventurers in D&D lore are noted to **tap objects with a 10-foot pole before touching them** — a classic dungeoneering practice that directly counters the Mimic.

## 8. Ecology / Variants

### Reproduction and Lifespan

- **Lifespan:** Unknown with certainty, but Mimics appear to be **very long-lived** — possibly centuries. A Mimic in a well-trafficked dungeon that feeds regularly may live indefinitely, barring violence.
- **Reproduction:** Mimics reproduce by **binary fission** in some editions — splitting into two smaller Mimics when they reach sufficient size. In 5e, the mechanism is left more vague. *Volo's Guide* suggests that large Mimics occasionally "bud" smaller Mimics, which then develop independently.
- **Growth:** Young Mimics are Small-sized and can only mimic smaller objects (a lockbox, a backpack, a bucket). As they feed and grow, they expand their size and the range of objects they can imitate.

### Variants

**From 5e lore (primarily Volo's Guide):**

| Variant | Description |
|---|---|
| **Mimic Colony** | Multiple Mimics working in concert. Can collectively cover an entire room — floor, walls, ceiling, furniture, all Mimic. When prey enters, everything attacks at once. Horrifying. Non-SRD. |
| **Large Mimic** | A Mimic that has grown to Large size over decades. Can mimic boats, wagons, large statues, gates. Dramatically more dangerous. |
| **Object Mimic (Tiny)** | A young or small Mimic that imitates very small objects — a coin pouch, a book, a single piece of fruit. Rare, usually found in urban settings. |
| **Psychic Mimic** | A Mimic that has absorbed enough intelligent prey to develop basic telepathy — can sense surface thoughts of approaching creatures to optimize its disguise. Non-SRD. |

**The Mimic Colony — The Standout Concept**

The Mimic Colony from *Volo's Guide* deserves special attention. The concept:
- A large group of Mimics develops a **collective intelligence** approaching sentience
- The colony coordinates to cover an entire room, so every surface is a Mimic
- When prey enters, the door (a Mimic) seals shut, the floor (Mimics) begins to heave, the walls (Mimics) develop mouths
- The colony can communicate and, in extreme cases, negotiate with intelligent creatures

This is not SRD content, but it represents the most extreme expression of the Mimic concept: not a single object trap, but a **location that is itself a predator**.

### Ecological Role

In dungeon ecosystems, Mimics are:
- **Passive checkpoints** — they don't disrupt the active ecology the way trolls or owlbears do
- **Inadvertent treasure hoarders** — accumulated loot from consumed adventurers makes old Mimic territories worth finding, if you can survive them
- **Opportunistic scavengers** of the dungeon economy — they benefit from other predators driving prey into treasure rooms

Other monsters have complex reactions to Mimics:
- **Most monsters ignore Mimics** that are in disguise — they know what the Mimic is and don't touch it
- **Some intelligent monsters use Mimics deliberately** — placing a Mimic in a treasure room as a guard, feeding it occasionally to keep it in place
- **Mimics occasionally form symbiotic relationships** with other dungeon creatures: a Mimic near a rust monster territory benefits because adventurers flee the rust monster into the Mimic's room

## 9. Interesting / Surprising Facts

1. **The 10-foot pole exists because of Mimics.** The classic dungeoneering tool — poking objects from a distance before touching them — was essentially invented as a Mimic countermeasure. In D&D culture, the 10-foot pole is shorthand for paranoid caution, and the Mimic is the reason.

2. **The loot is still there.** Old Mimics contain the indigestible gear of every adventurer they've ever eaten. A Mimic's body might have coins, rings, and magical items partially fused into it. Killing an old Mimic is like opening a dangerous piñata.

3. **A door can be a Mimic.** This is the most psychologically disturbing application. You reach for the door handle. The handle is a tooth. The door grabs you. The room you wanted to escape into was never there.

4. **The disguise is physical, not magical.** Detect Magic won't help you. The Mimic is genuinely changing its physical form — growing a wood-grain texture, a metal-colored surface, working hinges. It is biological shapeshifting, not illusion.

5. **They may be able to speak.** Very old, very well-fed Mimics have absorbed enough humanoid prey to acquire fragments of language. A Mimic that has been eating adventurers for 200 years might be able to whisper "hello" in several languages. Whether it chooses to use this as a lure or just does it instinctively is unknown.

6. **In some interpretations, the Mimic doesn't know what it is.** It doesn't have a concept of "I am pretending to be a chest." It simply is what it looks like, until something touches it and hunger fires. The self-awareness question is left open across editions — whether Mimics know they are mimics, or whether they simply exist in a state of being-object until triggered.

7. **Mimic colonies might be intelligent.** A group of Mimics that merge their consciousness becomes something smarter than any individual — and has been described in *Volo's Guide* as capable of rudimentary communication. A Mimic colony that develops full intelligence is one of the strangest possible D&D encounters: a room that can talk to you, and is debating whether to eat you.

8. **The most dangerous moment is before combat.** Unlike most monsters, the Mimic is at its most dangerous the instant before you know what it is. Once you know it's a Mimic, it becomes a relatively straightforward (if sticky) fight. The entire threat is in the ambush moment.

## 10. Recommended Reveal Sections

### Best Fit: **"abilities"** + **"strange"**

---

### 1. "abilities" — How Does It Trap You?

**Why it's the best fit:**

The Mimic's adhesive and shapeshifting are the single most important things about it. For kids ages 7-9, the reveal of *how the trap works* is irresistible: you reach for the chest, it grabs your hand, you can't let go, and now it's biting you. This is the "aha" moment of the creature — the mechanics of the ambush.

A well-designed reveal here would walk through the sequence: looks like a chest → you touch it → suddenly stuck → teeth appear → now what? The adhesive detail is viscerally understandable to kids (think of super glue, of stepping on a sticky trap), and the visual of teeth erupting from a wooden chest lid is unforgettable.

This also lets you address the "how do you beat it?" angle organically — you don't touch it first, you use a stick or a spell from a distance.

The abilities reveal is the **core reveal** for this creature. No other creature in the list has a "you're trapped before the fight starts" mechanic, so it's unique to the Mimic.

---

### 2. "strange" — Is the Whole Room a Mimic?

**Why it's the second-best fit:**

The Mimic Colony concept — where the floor, walls, ceiling, and all furniture are collectively one creature — is the single most "I have to tell someone this" fact about Mimics. For the target age group (7-9), the idea that you could walk into a room and the *entire room* is alive and hungry is perfectly calibrated nightmare-fuel-but-fun.

The reveal can be framed as: "Some adventurers have discovered something even scarier than a Mimic — a room that is *all* Mimics." You entered through a door that was a Mimic. The floor is a Mimic. The ceiling is a Mimic. Even the other chests are Mimics.

This is the "strange but true" angle: escalating the single-object concept to its logical extreme. It's also a great hook for imagination — kids will immediately start wondering if other things could be Mimics (spoiler: yes).

**Not selected and why:**

- **"weaknesses"** — The Mimic's weaknesses (slow, no range) are tactically interesting but not *surprising*. They don't have the jaw-drop quality of the adhesive reveal or the colony concept.
- **"diet"** — What it eats (adventurers) is interesting but secondary to how it catches them. The "loot is still inside it" detail is good but works better as a Did-You-Know than a full reveal.
- **"lair"** — Mimics don't have a traditional lair to reveal. The colony concept (already selected for "strange") is the closest equivalent.
- **"origin"** — No real-world mythology roots. Not applicable.
- **"signs"** — "How do you know a Mimic is there?" is a decent reveal, but it's a subset of the abilities reveal and doesn't stand alone as well.
- **"social"** — Mimics are solitary. The only interesting social angle is the colony, already covered.
- **"scale"** — Size (Medium) is not the notable thing about a Mimic. This works better for creatures where scale is surprising (Purple Worm, Hydra).
- **"alignment"** — Unaligned. Not interesting for a reveal section.
- **"survive"** — "How do Mimics survive?" is essentially the same question as abilities/diet. Better folded in.

## 11. Hebrew Name

The standard Hebrew rendering in Israeli D&D communities is **מִימִיק** (Mimik) — a direct transliteration, used commonly in translated content. However, a more evocative and meaningful name might better serve a kids' app:

- **מַחְקֶה** (Makhkeh) — "one who imitates/mimics" — from the root חקה (to imitate). Readable and descriptive.
- **מִתְחַפֵּשׂ** (Mitkhapes) — "one who disguises itself" — from the root חפש (to search, to disguise). More commonly used for "disguised" in modern Hebrew.
- **הַמְּלֶכֶדֶת** (HaMelekhedet) — "the trap-setter" — from מלכודת (trap). Evocative but slightly complex.

**Recommendation:** Use **מִימִיק** (Mimik) as the primary name (it's what kids who play D&D in Israel will know) with **מַחְקֶה** (Makhkeh) as an optional subtitle. Or simply transliterate as **מִימִיק** with nikud on the name, followed by "(Mimic)" in English — consistent with the app's naming convention.

## 12. Content Suggestions for the App

### For the Main Description (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1 — The Disguise:** Somewhere deep in the dungeon, there's a chest that looks like every other chest. Same wood, same iron bands, same lock. But this one is alive. It has been sitting perfectly still for weeks, waiting for someone to reach for it.

**Paragraph 2 — The Ambush:** The instant a hand touches the lid, the trap springs. The surface is covered in a sticky substance so powerful that nothing can pull free. A mouth tears open — rows of curved teeth biting down. What looked like a chest is now a predator, and you are already caught.

**Paragraph 3 — The Hunter:** Mimics live deep in dungeons and ruins, in rooms full of treasure, because they know that's where adventurers go. They don't chase prey. They don't need to. They choose their spot, become perfectly still, and wait. The most dangerous Mimic is one you never suspected was there at all.

### For "Did You Know?"
