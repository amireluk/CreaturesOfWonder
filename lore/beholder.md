# Beholder — D&D Lore Research

## Status Note

The Beholder is **NOT in the D&D 5e SRD**. It is listed as Wizards of the Coast "Product Identity" and explicitly excluded from the Open Game License content. All information below is drawn from:
- *Monster Manual* (5e, 2014)
- *Volo's Guide to Monsters* (5e, 2016)
- Earlier editions (1e Monster Manual, 2e, 3.5e, 4e) for historical/lore continuity
- D&D designer commentary and published sourcebooks

---

## 1. Origin / History

### Who Created the Beholder?

The Beholder is **one of the most original monsters in all of D&D** — it has no real-world mythological or folkloric precursor. It was invented by **Terry Kuntz** (brother of Rob Kuntz, a collaborator of Gary Gygax) and first appeared in *Greyhawk* (1975), one of the earliest D&D supplements. It was then included in the *Monster Manual* (1977).

Unlike creatures such as the Troll (Poul Anderson novel), Basilisk (classical antiquity), or Werewolf (European folklore), the Beholder has no identifiable real-world ancestor. There is no ancient myth of a floating many-eyed sphere. It is a wholly original D&D creation — which is part of why Wizards of the Coast claims it as Product Identity.

Some designers have noted loose thematic kinship with the **Evil Eye** concept from various world cultures (the belief that certain beings can harm or curse through their gaze), but no specific source creature inspired the design. The Evil Eye exists across Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and South Asian traditions — but those are single-eyed concepts, not the beholder's spectacular multi-eye architecture.

### Publication History

- **1975**: First appearance in *Greyhawk* supplement
- **1977**: *Monster Manual* (AD&D 1e) — fully statted
- Every subsequent edition has included it as a flagship monster
- **1991**: *I, Tyrant* — an entire sourcebook dedicated to beholder civilization and variants
- **2016**: *Volo's Guide to Monsters* gives extensive beholder lore and psychology — this is the richest single source for beholder behavior and ecology

The Beholder has appeared on the cover of multiple D&D products and is widely considered one of the three "signature monsters" of D&D alongside the Dragon and the Mind Flayer.

### No Real-World Roots — A Strength for This App

Because the Beholder has no real-world mythology, the "Origin" reveal section is **not applicable** here. There is no interesting folklore backstory to tell. This is pure D&D invention.

## 2. Appearance

### Overview

The Beholder is immediately recognizable and unlike any creature in real-world myth or nature. It is essentially a **floating sphere with teeth, one enormous eye, and ten smaller eyes on stalks.**

### Physical Description (5e Monster Manual)

- **Shape:** Large, roughly spherical body, slightly flattened on the bottom. Not perfectly round — a bit lumpy and irregular.
- **Size:** Approximately **4-5 feet in diameter** for the central body globe. With eyestalks extended, total "wingspan" is roughly 6-8 feet. The stat block lists it as **Large**.
- **Weight:** Despite size, floats via magical levitation — no wings, no visible propulsion.
- **Central Eye:** Occupies roughly the front third of the body. Enormous — nearly the full width of the torso. The eye is dramatic: a large iris (often green, yellow, or gold in canonical art), a vertically slit or round pupil, and a watery, alien intelligence behind it. Always open. Never fully closes (does not need to sleep normally; when it does "sleep" the central eye dims but doesn't shut).
- **Eyestalks:** Ten tentacle-like stalks emerge from the top and sides of the body, each ending in a smaller but functional eye. Eyestalks are flexible, semi-rigid, and roughly 1-3 feet long. They move independently, giving the beholder nearly 360-degree vision.
- **Mouth:** A wide, gaping mouth on the lower front of the sphere, filled with rows of jagged teeth. In Monster Manual art, the mouth is stretched and fang-filled, giving the creature its menacing grin. It can bite in melee, but this is rarely its first choice.
- **Skin:** Rough, bumpy, covered in chitinous plates, nodules, and irregular growths. Texture like stone or thick lizard hide. Color varies significantly:
  - Most commonly: **mottled purple, grey-brown, or olive green**
  - Underbelly tends to be slightly lighter/softer looking
  - Some beholders are nearly black, others pale grey
  - Volo's Guide notes that **no two beholders look exactly alike** — this matters to their psychology (see Behavior)
- **Underbelly:** The lower hemisphere is slightly smoother, often paler. This is where the body transitions to the mouth.
- **Movement:** Floats via natural levitation, roughly 4-5 feet off the ground typically. Can hover in place with perfect stability. Speed 0 ft walking, 20 ft flying.
- **No visible nose or ears** — senses are almost entirely through its eyes.
- **Smell:** Some sources suggest beholders have a faint acrid/chemical smell.

### What Makes It Visually Striking

The defining visual: that enormous central eye staring at you, ringed by eyestalks each independently scanning the room, teeth grinning below it. It looks like something from a nightmare — alien, wrong, impossible by natural biology. The floating adds to the uncanny quality.

## 3. Behavior & Psychology

This is where the Beholder truly stands apart. Volo's Guide to Monsters dedicates extensive pages to beholder psychology, and it is extraordinary.

### Ego and Supremacy

Beholders are **megalomaniacal in the extreme.** They believe, genuinely and without irony, that they are the most perfect beings in existence. Not "a great creature" — THE greatest creature. Their self-image is of absolute superiority over every other form of life.

This is not boastfulness. To a beholder, it is simply obvious fact. Other creatures exist either as tools (temporarily useful), food (eventually useful), or threats (to be destroyed preemptively). The concept of an equal does not exist in beholder cognition.

### Intelligence

Beholders are **exceptionally intelligent** — INT 17 in 5e. They are as smart as a brilliant human scholar, and often smarter. They can speak multiple languages (Common, Undercommon, and their own language, Deep Speech). They plan, scheme, manipulate, and remember. They have **perfect memory** for details that matter to them.

This makes them far more dangerous than a merely powerful monster. A beholder will study adventurers, identify their weaknesses, lure them into favorable positions, and deploy its eye rays with tactical precision.

### Paranoia

Beholders are **chronically, pathologically paranoid.** The universe, in a beholder's worldview, is full of things trying to destroy it. This is not entirely irrational — beholders do have many enemies, including other beholders. But the paranoia goes beyond any rational threat assessment.

A beholder will:
- Redesign its lair obsessively to eliminate blind spots and ambush approaches
- Suspect any visitor of being an assassin, even if they appear friendly
- Sometimes execute its own minions on suspicion of treachery
- Refuse to sleep normally because it fears being helpless

### Hatred of Other Beholders — The Most Remarkable Behavioral Trait

Here is the truly extraordinary fact, worth centering in the research:

**Beholders hate all other beholders.** Not as rivals. Not competitively. With genuine, passionate, visceral loathing.

The reason is this: a beholder believes it is the perfect form of existence. Therefore, any other beholder — which looks slightly different — is an **aberration, an abomination, a corruption of the ideal.** The existence of another beholder is an insult to the concept of the perfect beholder.

This means:
- Two beholders who encounter each other will almost always fight to the death
- Beholder cults and organizations (like the Eye Tyrant cults in the Forgotten Realms) require extraordinary circumstances to function — usually one beholder dominating others through sheer power
- A beholder's greatest enemy is often another beholder

In 50 years of D&D, this has remained one of the most fascinating pieces of monster psychology ever written. It's the kind of detail that makes the world feel coherent and alien at the same time.

### Xenophobia Generally

The beholder's xenophobia extends to all life, not just other beholders. **It hates being near things that are different from itself.** Because everything is different from a beholder, it essentially hates everything. Other creatures are tolerated only as long as they serve a purpose.

### Solitary but Commanding

Beholders are **solitary by strong preference.** However, their paranoia combined with intelligence leads them to maintain **thrall networks** — groups of enslaved or dominated creatures who serve as guards, servants, and early-warning systems. These thralls are not companions. They are tools. The beholder feels nothing for them.

Typical beholder thrall roster includes: grimlocks (blind cave humanoids), gricks, ogres, and humanoids charmed by the beholder's eye rays. More powerful beholders may command entire underground networks spanning multiple dungeon levels.

### Dreams and Madness

One of the most fascinating pieces of beholder lore from Volo's Guide:

**Beholders dream. And their dreams can create new beholders.**

When a beholder sleeps (which they resist doing, and do rarely), they can spawn **dream duplicates** — beholders spawned from the original's nightmares or fantasy. These "dream beholders" are often twisted variants that reflect the original's subconscious obsessions. This is canon D&D explanation for why beholder variants exist: they literally dreamed each other into being.

This explains:
- Why beholder variants (Death Tyrant, Spectator, Death Kiss, Gauth) exist
- Why each beholder looks slightly different
- Why beholders are so disturbed by meeting their variants: the variant is proof of their own subconscious imperfection

## 4. Habitat

### Primary: Underground

Beholders live almost exclusively **underground** — deep caves, dungeon complexes, cavern systems. They are creatures of total darkness and complete control.

The underground preference is partly practical (no weather, limited access points to defend) and mostly psychological: **a beholder wants a space it can fully control.** The surface world is chaotic, exposed, and full of variables the beholder cannot dominate.

### The Lair

A beholder's lair is its masterwork — a reflection of its obsessive need for security and control.

Key lair features (from Volo's Guide):
- **Multiple levels connected by shafts** — beholders fly, so vertical movement is natural. Stairs are for thralls. Shafts allow the beholder to move freely through its lair while making pursuit difficult for walking creatures.
- **Minimal flat surfaces** — beholders distrust flat floors. Many lairs have tiered ledges, pits, and irregular terrain that disadvantages ground-bound intruders.
- **A treasure chamber** that the beholder obsesses over. Beholders are hoarders — they collect items that represent their conquests and superiority.
- **Sleeping chamber** in the deepest, most defended location. When a beholder sleeps (rarely), it wants maximum protection.
- **Thrall quarters** — areas where dominated creatures live, serve, and act as early warning systems and cannon fodder.
- **The "throne room"** concept — a central chamber where the beholder holds court and deals with visitors (before usually killing them).

Beholders choose their lair locations with great care and often spend years improving them. Moving a beholder from its lair is nearly impossible by diplomacy — only overwhelming force works.

### Ecology of Underground Spaces

In dungeon ecology, a beholder is a **keystone predator** — its presence structures the entire local food chain. Other predators either serve it, flee it, or avoid the area entirely. A beholder's influence zone can span an entire dungeon level or more.

## 5. Diet

### What They Eat

Beholders are **carnivores** with a strong preference for **fresh, killed prey.** They do not eat plants, fungi, or carrion if they can help it (though they can if necessary).

Primary prey:
- Humanoids (humans, elves, dwarves — whatever wanders into their territory)
- Their own thralls, when the thralls cease to be useful or become suspicious
- Cave creatures (ropers, grells, whatever shares their underground space)
- Other aberrations they can defeat

Beholders eat using their mouth (the lower part of the sphere, full of teeth). The teeth are genuine — they can bite in melee combat for real damage.

### How They Hunt

Beholders do not typically **hunt** in the sense of pursuing prey through the wilderness. Their approach is more lordly:

1. **Thralls do the hunting.** Commanded creatures capture or drive prey toward the beholder.
2. **Ambush from above.** Beholders can float near the ceiling of large caverns, invisible in the dark (they have darkvision 120 feet), and catch prey off-guard.
3. **Eye-ray incapacitation first.** The beholder will typically paralyze, slow, or hold prey with its eye rays before approaching to feed.
4. **Territorial interception.** Anything entering the beholder's lair is functionally entering its pantry.

### Food as Power Expression

Interestingly, beholders sometimes keep living prey for extended periods — not out of mercy, but because it demonstrates their power. A held prisoner is evidence that the beholder is so powerful it need not even hurry with the killing.

## 6. Abilities & Combat

### The Central Eye — Anti-Magic Cone

The **central eye** is the beholder's most powerful and most defining ability. It projects a **60-foot cone of anti-magic** in whatever direction the beholder is facing.

Within this cone:
- **No spells can be cast** (by anyone, including the beholder itself — the cone affects the beholder's front hemisphere too)
- **Active spells are suppressed** (magic items stop working, concentration spells end, magical effects are suspended)
- **The effect is constant and passive** — as long as the beholder's central eye is open, the cone exists
- The beholder controls the cone's direction by turning its body

Strategic implications:
- Spellcasters are neutralized if the beholder faces them
- The beholder must choose: point its anti-magic cone at the spellcasters (disabling them) or away (allowing it to use its own eye rays from the eyestalks in that direction)
- **This creates a genuine tactical puzzle** — a party with multiple threats forces the beholder into constant repositioning decisions

The central eye **cannot be closed voluntarily** while the beholder is awake. It is always on. When a beholder sleeps (dimly, partially), the cone weakens.

### The Ten Eyestalk Rays

Each eyestalk fires a different magical ray. The beholder chooses up to 3 rays per round (5e), targeting any creature it can see (not within the anti-magic cone, since magic is suppressed there).

Here are all ten eyestalk ray effects from the 5e Monster Manual:

| # | Ray Name | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Charm Ray** | Target charmed by beholder for 1 hour (Wisdom save). Treats beholder as a trusted friend. |
| 2 | **Paralyzing Ray** | Target paralyzed for 1 minute (Constitution save). Can't move or act. |
| 3 | **Fear Ray** | Target frightened for 1 minute (Wisdom save). Must flee from the beholder. |
| 4 | **Slowing Ray** | Target speed halved, -2 AC and Dex saves, can't use reactions (Constitution save). Lasts 1 minute. |
| 5 | **Enervation Ray** | Target takes 8d8 necrotic damage (Constitution save), half on save. Drains life force. |
| 6 | **Telekinetic Ray** | Beholder moves target up to 30 feet (Strength save). Can fling into pits, into walls, onto spikes. |
| 7 | **Sleep Ray** | Target unconscious for 1 minute (Wisdom save). Rendering them helpless for feeding. |
| 8 | **Petrification Ray** | Target restrained, then turned to stone permanently (Constitution saves). Two-stage process. |
| 9 | **Disintegration Ray** | Target takes 10d4+5 force damage (Dexterity save); if reduced to 0 HP, disintegrated into dust. Objects also disintegrated. |
| 10 | **Death Ray** | Target dies outright on failed save (Dexterity save); takes 10d10 radiant damage on success. The instant-kill ray. |

**Important mechanical note:** Eye rays are magical, so they do NOT work within the beholder's own anti-magic cone. The beholder must fire its eyestalk rays in directions where its central eye is NOT pointing.

### Legendary Resistance (5e)

3 times per day, if the beholder fails a saving throw, it can choose to succeed instead. This is the "veteran villain" mechanic — a boss monster that can shrug off some effects.

### Lair Actions

In its lair, a beholder gets additional actions each round (Lair Actions):
- Random eye ray fires at a random creature (the lair itself seems to "help" the beholder)
- Magical grasping appendages from the walls (in some versions)
- Dungeon terrain shifts

### Combat Behavior

Beholders are **not berserkers.** They fight intelligently:
1. **Open at range** — fire eye rays to disable the most dangerous threats first (Death Ray or Disintegration at spellcasters or heavily armored melee fighters)
2. **Use Charm Ray** on one target to turn them against their allies
3. **Rotate to manage the anti-magic cone** — keep facing the spellcasters to neutralize their magic
4. **Use Telekinetic Ray to control the battlefield** — throw enemies into pits, off ledges, into each other
5. **Reserve Death Ray for the most dangerous opponent**
6. **Fall back, never pursue** — if badly injured, retreat to a defensible position. It will not give up its life for pride.

### Full 5e Stat Block Summary

- **CR:** 13 (10,000 XP)
- **AC:** 18 (natural armor)
- **HP:** 180 (19d10 + 76)
- **Speed:** 0 ft. walking, 20 ft. flying (hover)
- **STR** 10, **DEX** 14, **CON** 18, **INT** 17, **WIS** 15, **CHA** 17
- **Saving Throws:** Int +8, Wis +7, Cha +8
- **Skills:** Perception +12 (due to so many eyes)
- **Condition Immunities:** Prone (floating — can't be knocked down)
- **Senses:** Darkvision 120 ft.
- **Languages:** Deep Speech, Undercommon
- **Legendary Actions:** 3 per round (eye ray, attack, and other options)

## 7. Weaknesses

### The Anti-Magic Cone Cuts Both Ways

This is the crucial, somewhat ironic weakness: **the beholder cannot use its magical eye rays in the same direction as its anti-magic cone.** Magic doesn't work in the cone, including its own.

If the beholder is pointing its central eye at a group of enemies, it cannot simultaneously fire eyestalk rays in that direction. It must choose: neutralize their magic, or blast them with rays. This creates genuine tactical counterplay.

A clever party can exploit this:
- **Spellcasters stay in front** — force the beholder to keep its anti-magic cone forward (neutralizing its rays in that direction) while melee fighters attack from the sides or behind
- This "splitting" of the beholder's threat management is the key to beating it

### Specific Vulnerabilities

1. **Anti-magic cone self-restriction** — as described above: the beholder is its own worst enemy in terms of the cone
2. **No physical armor** — high AC from natural hide, but vulnerable to sustained physical damage (it has 180 HP but no damage resistances in 5e, unlike many CR 13+ creatures)
3. **Overconfidence / ego** — beholders are predictable in their arrogance. They believe their defenses are impenetrable; smart players can exploit this
4. **Cannot be prone** but can be blinded — if you can blind a beholder, ALL of its abilities (including the anti-magic cone, which requires it to be able to see) are compromised
5. **Dreams and sleeping** — when a beholder does sleep, it is genuinely vulnerable. Its anti-magic cone weakens, its eye rays stop functioning fully, and it can be caught. This is extremely rare to exploit but exists in the lore
6. **Hatred of other beholders** — can theoretically be exploited. If you can get one beholder to believe another is nearby, the paranoia can be weaponized

### No Elemental Weaknesses

Unlike the Troll (fire) or Basilisk (reflections), there is no single "trick" to beating a beholder. **It is meant to be one of the hardest fights in D&D.** CR 13 is extremely high. The beholder is a potential TPK (Total Party Kill) situation for parties that don't approach it carefully.

## 8. Interesting / Surprising Facts

### The Dreaming Beholder

The single most remarkable fact about beholders: **beholders dream, and their dreams can become real beholders.**

When a beholder sleeps, its subconscious manifests as other beholders — variants that reflect its fears, fantasies, and obsessions. A beholder that fears death might dream a **Death Tyrant** (undead beholder). A beholder fascinated with watching might dream a **Spectator** (smaller, four-eyed beholder variant).

This is Volo's Guide lore, and it has a wonderful internal consistency: it explains why beholder variants exist (they're dreamed into existence), why each beholder looks slightly different (they're each someone's dream), and why beholders hate their variants so much (the variant is evidence of the original's own subconscious imperfection).

### No Two Are Alike

Because beholders may all trace back to dreams and sub-creations, **no two beholders look exactly alike.** The number of eyestalks can vary slightly, the skin patterns differ, the size of the central eye varies. This matters to beholder psychology: a beholder cannot even look at another beholder without seeing a distorted, wrong version of what it considers perfect (itself).

### Perfect Memory, Obsessive Planning

A beholder never forgets a slight, a face, or a tactical failure. They will plan revenge across decades. They do not forgive, and they do not move on. In campaign play, a beholder that a party injures but fails to kill will become a **recurring nemesis** that has spent months redesigning its lair and developing specific countermeasures against the party's known abilities.

### They Hate Being Looked At

This is a delicious irony: a creature made entirely of eyes **hates being stared at.** Being observed by another creature (especially another beholder, or a scrying spell) sends a beholder into paroxysms of paranoid rage. Their eyes are weapons. Other creatures' eyes are threats.

### Beholder Speech

Beholders speak **Deep Speech** — the alien language of aberrations and Far Realm entities. It is described as deeply unsettling to hear: a combination of sounds, non-verbal frequencies, and psychic impressions that non-aberrations find disturbing. They also speak Undercommon (the trade language of the Underdark).

### The Spectator Variant — An Accidental Summoning

One variant, the **Spectator**, is not a dreamed beholder but a summoned one. Spellcasters can summon Spectators to guard treasure or locations. A Spectator assigned a guard duty will fulfill that duty for 101 years — after which, if the treasure or location no longer exists, it goes insane from the lack of purpose. This is a fascinating piece of lore: an aberrant creature so invested in its purpose that it breaks mentally without one.

## 9. Ecology & Variants

### Beholder Variants (from Volo's Guide and Monster Manual)

| Variant | Description |
|---|---|
| **Death Tyrant** | An undead beholder — when a beholder dies in extreme paranoia (fearing death so intensely it transcends it), it can return as a Death Tyrant. Its central eye now projects a ray of death rather than anti-magic. CR 14. |
| **Spectator** | Smaller (4 eyestalks instead of 10), summoned rather than born, bound to guard duty. CR 3. Much weaker. |
| **Gauth** | A "lesser beholder" — smaller, weaker, meaner, less intelligent. Often considered an imperfect/failed beholder. CR 6. |
| **Death Kiss** | A beholder variant whose eyestalks end in mouths with teeth instead of eyes — used for blood-draining. Nightmare fuel. CR 10. |
| **Ghost Beholder** | Ghostly undead beholder. Rare. |
| **Elder Orb** | Ancient, highly powerful beholder variant. Accumulated centuries of knowledge. CR 15+. |

The dreaming-creates-variants lore means this list is functionally open-ended. Any beholder nightmare could produce a new variant.

### Beholder Society (or Lack of It)

Beholders do not have "society" in any conventional sense. However, in the Forgotten Realms setting, there are:

- **The Xanathar Guild** in Waterdeep — run by a beholder called the Xanathar. This is a rare case of a beholder successfully using thralls and dominated creatures to build an organization. The Xanathar is notable for being genuinely attached to its goldfish (named Sylgar), which is the most humanizing detail in beholder lore and a beloved D&D fact.
- **Eye Tyrant networks** — informal territories where particularly powerful beholders establish dominance over weaker ones
- **Beholder cults** — in some settings, beholders are worshipped by humanoid cults who bring them offerings in exchange for protection (or simply to avoid being eaten)

### Beholders and the Far Realm

Beholders are classified as **Aberrations** in D&D — creatures that are fundamentally alien to the natural world. Their origin, in deep lore, traces to the **Far Realm** — a plane of existence so alien that it operates by different physical and logical laws. Aberrations are things from that place, or things contaminated by it.

This gives beholders a metaphysical quality: they are not just strange animals. They are intrusions of wrongness into the natural world. Their very existence distorts things around them. This is why their dreams create new beholders — they are creatures of impossible logic, and impossible things happen around them.

The Far Realm connection is most prominent in the Forgotten Realms and is deliberately kept vague — the designers want the Far Realm and its creatures to remain incomprehensible.

### Underground Ecosystem Role

In dungeon ecology, a beholder functions as an **apex controller** — not just predator, but organizer. Unlike a purple worm (which just eats everything), a beholder will:
- Establish organized thrall hierarchies
- Negotiate (briefly, strategically) with other powerful creatures before eventually dominating or killing them
- Create "buffer zones" of neutral territory around its core domain
- Effectively run a feudal underground territory with itself as absolute monarch

This makes a beholder's lair a more complex and interesting dungeon than a simple monster cave. There are politics, hierarchies, and opportunities for non-combat interaction before the inevitable combat.

## 10. Recommended Reveal Sections

### Best Fit: **"Special Abilities"** + **"Strange but True"**

**Reasoning:**

1. **"Special Abilities"** — The ten eye rays are the defining feature of the beholder beyond its appearance. For kids ages 7-9, the idea that each eye does something different (one puts you to sleep, one turns you to stone, one makes you dust) is absolutely captivating. This is the beholder's "cool mechanic" that begs to be revealed. Focus on 4-5 of the most evocative ones (Sleep, Petrify, Disintegrate, Death, Charm) — no need to enumerate all ten.

2. **"Strange but True"** — The dreaming fact. *"When a beholder sleeps, it can dream new beholders into existence."* This single sentence is the most memorable, most surprising, most "I have to tell someone this" fact about the beholder. It perfectly fits the "strange but true" reveal type. It's kid-accessible (everyone knows about dreams), conceptually surprising, and requires no rules knowledge to appreciate.

**Not a good fit:**
- "Origin" — there IS no interesting real-world mythology. The beholder is pure D&D invention.
- "Weaknesses" — the anti-magic cone self-limitation is too mechanically complex for 7-9 year olds in a reveal context
- "How big are they really?" — their size is notable but not their most interesting quality
- "Pack or loner?" — loner, yes, but the more interesting angle is the "hates other beholders" piece, which fits better in the main description or did-you-know

## 11. Hebrew Name

The standard Hebrew name for Beholder in Israeli D&D contexts is **בּוֹחֵן** (Bocheyn / Bo'chen) — literally "the one who examines/scrutinizes" — an apt translation. Some sources use **צוֹפֶה** (Tzofeh, "the watcher") or simply transliterate as **בִּיהוֹלְדֶר**.

The most common and resonant choice for this app would likely be **הַבּוֹחֵן** (HaBocheyn, "The Scrutinizer/Examiner") — it captures the creature's essence (all-seeing, examining, judging everything) and sounds appropriately ominous in Hebrew.

Alternative worth considering: **עֵין הָרֶשַׁע** (Ayin HaResha, "The Evil Eye") — evocative, culturally resonant in Hebrew, though it shifts the creature's character toward the folkloric Evil Eye rather than the D&D Beholder specifically.

**Recommendation:** Use **בּוֹחֵן** (Bocheyn) as the Hebrew name — it's the most established translation and captures the creature's core identity as a scrutinizing, all-observing, judging entity.

## 12. Content Suggestions for the App

### For the Main Description (3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1 — Appearance:** The floating sphere, the enormous central eye, the ten independent eyestalks, the teeth-filled mouth. What it looks like to first see one.

**Paragraph 2 — Psychology/Behavior:** It believes it is perfect. It hates everything that is different from it — including other beholders. It is brilliant, paranoid, and completely alone by choice.

**Paragraph 3 — Danger/Habitat:** It lives deep underground, in elaborately designed lairs. It is one of the most dangerous creatures in the D&D world. CR 13.

### For "Did You Know?"

The Xanathar's goldfish. *"The most famous beholder in the world, called the Xanathar, is the crime lord of a whole city — and its most prized possession is a goldfish named Sylgar."* This is simultaneously surprising, humanizing, funny, and memorable for a kid.

Alternative: The dreaming fact in one sentence.
