# Gelatinous Cube — Research Notes

## D&D Stats & Mechanics
- **CR**: 2 — dangerous for low-level adventurers but not catastrophic
- **Size**: Large — exactly 10 feet on every side (the defining constraint)
- **Habitat**: Underground dungeons and cave systems exclusively
- **Danger**: 2/5 — the primary danger is not noticing it, not raw power
- **Key abilities**:
  - **Transparent**: nearly invisible; DC 15 Perception to notice before walking into it
  - **Engulf**: creatures hit are engulfed — restrained inside the body, taking 6d6 acid damage per turn
  - **Paralysis**: engulfed creatures must make a DC 12 Con save or be paralyzed
  - **Ooze Cube**: occupies its entire space; creatures can enter it (accidentally or forcibly)
  - Immune to being blinded, charmed, deafened, exhausted, frightened, prone
  - No intelligence (INT 1) — completely mindless
- **Speed**: 15 ft — very slow
- **No attack roll for engulf**: you don't fight a gelatinous cube; you accidentally walk into one

## Behavior & Ecology
- Mindless — no goals, no hunger signals, no awareness of anything beyond "move and dissolve"
- Moves slowly through dungeon corridors in a straight path, changing direction when it hits a wall
- Perfectly fills a 10×10 corridor — this is not a coincidence, it's a predator shaped for its environment
- Does not hunt; it ambles. Creatures that fail to notice it walk directly into it.
- Dissolves all organic matter: flesh, leather, wood, rope. Metal and stone pass through undigested.
- Acts as a dungeon's natural cleaning system — consumes corpses, vermin, refuse
- Often contains visible remnants of past meals: bones, coins, weapons, armor pieces, floating suspended inside its body
- Reproduces by splitting when large enough — two smaller cubes

## Real-World Origins
- **100% D&D original** — no real-world mythology, no folklore precedent, no natural analogue
- First appeared in the original D&D supplement *Greyhawk* (1975) by Gary Gygax
- Designed as a "dungeon ecology" creature — something that explains why dungeons aren't just full of bones and refuse
- The concept of an invisible corridor-filling cube that you walk into accidentally is pure Gygax absurdist game design
- No origin section warranted — this creature's story IS its D&D origin, not mythology

## Interesting Facts
- A gelatinous cube is so transparent it's nearly invisible — experienced adventurers learn to look for the floating debris inside, not the cube itself
- The bones and items visible floating inside a cube are its "wanted poster" — if you see a rusty sword hovering at chest height with nothing around it, run
- It takes 6 hours for a gelatinous cube to fully dissolve a human body
- Metal objects (swords, coins, armor) pass through the cube without dissolving — they stay suspended inside until the cube is destroyed, then fall to the floor
- Thieves and adventurers have learned to destroy gelatinous cubes specifically to recover the valuables floating inside them

## Reveal Section Candidates

1. **`signs`** — "How to spot it before you walk in" is THE defining challenge of this creature. A 10-foot invisible cube in a corridor is a setup; the reveal of how to detect it is satisfying and practical. Kids will love the "floating sword trick." **→ RECOMMENDED**
2. **`strange`** — The cube is genuinely one of the weirdest creature concepts in existence. Why is it a cube? Why is it see-through? Why does it fit perfectly in a hallway? This creature deserves a "weirdest thing about it" section. **→ RECOMMENDED**
3. **`diet`** — How it eats is interesting (acid that dissolves organic matter, metals float through), but this overlaps heavily with `strange` content and is better folded in there.

**Recommendation**: `signs` + `strange`. These are the two questions kids will have about this creature, and together they tell the full story of what makes it so unique.
